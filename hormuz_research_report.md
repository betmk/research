# Strait of Hormuz Crisis: Oil Supply Disruption Briefing

**Date:** March 17, 2026
**Status:** Crisis ongoing — Strait effectively closed since Mar 5; first test transit Mar 16

---

## Executive Summary

The US-Israel war on Iran (Feb 28, 2026) triggered the largest oil supply disruption in history. Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz has taken ~20 million barrels/day (mb/d) of transit offline — roughly 20% of global daily oil supply. Combined mitigating factors (bypass pipelines, SPR releases, Russian supply) cover only ~6 mb/d, leaving a **net shortage of ~14 mb/d**. Brent crude has surged ~40% to $100+/bbl. Extended well shut-ins risk permanent damage if the crisis exceeds 4 weeks.

## Repair & Restart Timelines

### Physical Damage (Drone/Missile Strikes)
- **Ras Tanura refinery:** 2-4 weeks (fire damage, equipment inspection required)
- **Ras Laffan LNG:** "At least 2 weeks" per Qatar's energy minister — LNG facilities are highly complex with strict safety protocols
- **Fujairah terminal:** Under assessment; fire damage complicates ADCOP bypass value
- **Others (Duqm, Salalah, Mussafah):** Weeks to assess and repair; fuel storage tank replacement is 4-8 weeks minimum

### Shut-in Wells (No Physical Damage, but Idled)
- **First 2 weeks:** Restart is relatively straightforward — days to a week per well
- **2-4 weeks idle:** Corrosion begins; requires inspection and potential equipment replacement before restart
- **Beyond 4 weeks:** **Risk of permanent well damage** — oil can become trapped in subsurface, making it permanently unrecoverable. Equipment failures and geological breakdowns accelerate.
- **IEA assessment:** "Upstream production will take weeks and, in some cases, months, to return to pre-crisis levels"

### Strait of Hormuz Reopening
- **ING base case:** 4 weeks total disruption (2 weeks full closure + 2 weeks at 50%)
- **Bull case:** First test transit on Mar 16 suggests possible negotiated safe passage; could accelerate if US assembles coalition escort
- **Bear case:** Iran's new supreme leader has stated Hormuz "must remain closed" — could extend for months if regime hardens

## Mitigating Factors: What's Filling the Gap

### 1. Saudi East-West Pipeline (Petroline)
- **Pipe capacity:** 7 mb/d (after Mar 11 NGL line conversion)
- **Yanbu port bottleneck:** 4.5 mb/d nominal, ~3 mb/d wartime estimate
- **Saudi domestic refinery consumption:** ~2 mb/d at Yanbu
- **Net new export capacity:** **~1.0-1.5 mb/d**
- **Duration:** Indefinite (infrastructure, not a consumable)
- **Limitation:** Only moves crude oil — no refined products, LPG, or LNG

### 2. UAE ADCOP Pipeline (Habshan-Fujairah)
- **Pipe capacity:** 1.5-1.8 mb/d
- **Current utilization:** ~71% (~1.1 mb/d)
- **Spare capacity:** **~0.4-0.7 mb/d**
- **Duration:** Indefinite
- **Risk:** Fujairah terminal itself was hit by drone debris on Mar 2 — operational status uncertain

### 3. IEA Strategic Petroleum Reserve Release
- **Total committed:** 400 million barrels (largest in IEA's 50-year history)
- **Release rate:** ~2.0 mb/d max (practical IEA limit)
  - US: 172M bbl at 1.43 mb/d over 120 days (~Jul 10 exhaustion)
  - Japan: 80M bbl
  - Germany: 19.7M bbl
  - UK: 13.5M bbl
  - Others: remainder across 28 other member states
- **Duration:** 120-200 days depending on member
- **Reality check:** 400M bbl = only ~4 days of global consumption; IEA calls these "stop-gap measures"
- **Post-SPR:** If crisis extends past Jul 2026, US reserves will be deeply depleted

### 4. Russian Oil Increase
- **Incremental supply:** ~0.3-0.5 mb/d
- **Current exports:** 3.26 mb/d (4-week avg through Mar 8)
- **Price shift:** Was selling at $10-13/bbl discount; now at $4-5/bbl **premium**
- **Revenue impact:** +14% daily oil revenue vs. February
- **US response:** Temporarily eased some Russian oil sanctions to increase flow
- **Key role:** Russia is one of few heavy crude producers (along with Iran, Venezuela) — refineries designed for heavy crude have no substitute
- **Duration:** Ongoing as long as prices justify it

### 5. Iranian Own Exports
- **Volume:** ~1.5 mb/d on Iranian/allied flagged vessels
- **Status:** Iran controls the strait and continues its own shipments
- **Buyers:** Primarily China and India
- **Duration:** Ongoing

### 6. Combined Bypass Capacity vs. Gap

| Mitigation | Volume (mb/d) | Start Date | Est. Duration |
|-----------|---------------|------------|---------------|
| Saudi Pipeline (net export) | +1.5 | Mar 11 | Indefinite |
| UAE ADCOP spare | +0.5 | Mar 1 | Indefinite |
| IEA SPR release | +2.0 | Mar 12 | ~120 days (US) |
| Russian increase | +0.4 | Mar 3 | Ongoing |
| Iranian own exports | +1.5 | Ongoing | Ongoing |

| **NET SHORTAGE** | **-14.1 mb/d** | | |

**The gap is simply too large to fill without reopening the strait.** Bypass pipelines were designed for short, partial disruptions — not a full closure of the world's most critical oil chokepoint.

## What to Watch

1. **Coalition escort formation:** Trump is pressuring allies to help protect tanker traffic. If a credible naval escort materializes, partial Hormuz flow could resume.
2. **4-week shut-in threshold (~Mar 28):** Wells shut since Mar 3 approach the permanent damage window. Every week past this point means longer recovery and potentially lost capacity.
3. **Iran's new leadership:** The successor to Khamenei will set the tone on whether the strait closure is a bargaining chip or a long-term strategy.
4. **SPR drawdown pace:** At 2 mb/d, the collective 400M barrel reserve lasts ~200 days. The US portion (172M) is gone by mid-July at current release rates.
5. **Refined products crunch:** Bypass pipelines only move crude. Diesel, jet fuel, naphtha, and LPG shortages will worsen independently — Ras Laffan's LNG suspension alone affects 20% of global LNG supply.
6. **China/India positioning:** Both are buying Iranian and Russian crude at volume. Their stance on sanctions and conflict resolution matters.
