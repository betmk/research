Iran's Selective Blockade Strategy

Iran is not enforcing a total closure. It is operating a selective blockade — blocking US/Western-allied shipping while allowing passage for strategically useful traffic.[5] This divides the Western coalition (India and China have less incentive to act), maintains Iran's revenue, and turns Hormuz into a diplomatic lever. Traffic has collapsed 95%+ from the pre-war 100+ ships/day.[17]

Repair & Restart Timelines

Physical Damage

  • Ras Tanura: 2–4 weeks (fire damage, equipment inspection).[27]
  • Ras Laffan LNG: 3–5 YEARS for 12.8M mt/yr (17% of Qatar's capacity — two trains + one GTL facility) per QatarEnergy CEO (Mar 19); ~$20B/yr lost revenue; specialized LNG super-cooling equipment must be rebuilt; work cannot begin until hostilities cease; North Field expansion delayed to at least 2027.[60][61]
  • Fujairah: Hit 3 times; loading impaired but ADCOP pipeline flowing at 1.8 mb/d.[8]
  • South Pars / Assaluyeh: 4 gas treatment facilities offline (phases 3–6); fire controlled but no production timeline given; Tehran refinery (220K b/d) damaged separately.[42]
  • Others: Weeks to assess; fuel storage tank replacement 4–8 weeks minimum.[27]

Shut-in Wells

Water Coning Risk Wells shut >4 weeks face permanent damage. Bottom water rushes into mature reservoirs during extended shut-ins, trapping oil in pore spaces — potentially irreversible formation damage.[7] Wells shut since Mar 3 hit this critical window around Mar 28.
  • First 2 weeks: Restart straightforward (days to a week per well)
  • 2–4 weeks idle: Corrosion begins; inspection and equipment replacement needed
  • Beyond 4 weeks: Risk of permanent well damage — oil trapped in pore spaces, potentially unrecoverable
  • IEA: "Upstream production will take weeks and, in some cases, months, to return to pre-crisis levels."[1]

Strait Reopening Scenarios

  • ING base case: 4 weeks total (2 full closure + 2 at 50%).
  • Bull case: Selective passage already occurring; US Navy estimates escort readiness by end of March (needs 8–10 destroyers per convoy).[38]
  • Bear case: Mojtaba Khamenei hardliner; Iran FM "we never asked for a ceasefire"[6]; South Pars strike risks full-scale energy war — Iranian official warned it "triggers full-scale economic war."[47]

Post-Reopening Recovery Lag

2–2.5 Months to Steady State Even after Hormuz reopens, the system does not normalize immediately. Well restarts, pipeline recommissioning, insurance market normalization, and tanker fleet repositioning create a 2–2.5 month lag before Gulf supply returns to pre-crisis levels.[27]
  • Week 1–2: Selective vessels begin transit; war risk premiums remain elevated (1–2% of hull); tanker fleet repositioning underway
  • Week 2–4: Well restarts (those not permanently damaged); pipeline recommissioning; port congestion clears
  • Month 2: Insurance market begins normalizing; more tankers return to route; refinery crude slates rebalance
  • Month 2–2.5: Supply returns to 80–85% of pre-crisis levels; North Sea, US, Russian substitute barrels begin unwinding
  • Persistent damage: North Field expansion delayed to 2027[33]; wells damaged by water coning potentially unrecoverable; South Pars phases 3–6 restart timeline unknown.

3. IEA Strategic Petroleum Reserve POLICY

  • Total committed: 400M bbl — largest coordinated release in IEA's 50-year history[1][9]
  • Structure: First tranche is 86M bbl exchange (loan; must be returned with interest)
  • Rate: ~2.0 mb/d combined (US: 1.43 mb/d over 120 days; IEA non-US: 0.57 mb/d)[1]
  • US SPR capacity: 714M bbl total; held 415.4M bbl pre-release (peaked at 695M bbl in 2010)[1]
  • OECD government stocks: ~1.25B bbl total across IEA members[1]
  • Global observed stocks: ~8,210M bbl (IEA Jan 2026 estimate; highest since early 2021)[44]
  • SPR refill = structural price floor: 172M bbl committed requires ~$17B in repurchases at $100/bbl — a sustained government buy program supporting prices long after the crisis ends
  • Market reaction: Flat. Brent held $100+ on announcement day. Insufficient to offset −12 mb/d structural gap.[6]

4. GL 134: Russian Oil Waiver (One-Time) POLICY

Cliff Risk: Apr 11 GL 134 is a STOCK, not a FLOW. 186M bbl on ~238 tankers depletes and vanishes after expiration.[3][44] No extension signals as of Mar 18. This is the single most predictable near-term catalyst for price spikes.
  • OFAC GL 134: Authorizes delivery of Russian crude already loaded as of Mar 12[3]
  • Effective flow: ~2.0 mb/d tapering; 60–70M bbl near India/China for prompt delivery[44]
  • Expires: April 11, 2026 (30 days from issuance)
  • Russia windfall: Urals swung from −$13/bbl discount to +$4–5/bbl premium (an $18/bbl swing); India's Russian crude imports up ~50% to 1.5 mb/d; Russia earning ~$150M/day additional revenue[44][52]

5. Other Mitigations

  • Russian production: +0.1 mb/d (minimal; Feb production falling −56K bpd m/m; may cut 300K bpd by April per Rystad)
  • Iranian own exports: +1.5 mb/d via Kharg + Jask terminals, almost entirely China-bound[10]

6. Policy Measures

  • Jones Act waiver: Under consideration — would allow foreign-flagged tankers to move crude between US ports, freeing Gulf Coast refinery capacity. Politically sensitive (longshore labor opposition); last waived post-Katrina 2005.[28]
  • US crude export ban: Congressional Democrats proposing emergency ban on US crude exports. Would spike WTI vs. Brent; alienates allies dependent on 2–3 mb/d of US exports; opposed by producers and Treasury.[28]
  • CME Group warning: Exchange described potential $200+ Brent as a "biblical disaster for the global economy" — prompting review of emergency margin and circuit-breaker protocols.[11]

Petrochemical Cascade

  • Steam cracker physics: Crackers operate at a minimum ~60% utilization or must shut down entirely — no idle mode. A cold shutdown requires 2+ months to restart, creating downstream disruptions that outlast the oil crisis itself. ~20% of Asian ethylene capacity is currently under force majeure.[44]
  • Asian naphtha dependency: Over 60% of Asian naphtha is sourced from the Middle East — blocked Hormuz transit is simultaneously a crude shortage and a petrochemical feedstock crisis.[44]
  • LPG yield collapse: When crude slates shift from light Gulf grades to heavier alternatives, LPG yield drops from ~2% to ~1% of feedstock — compounding the direct LPG shortage from blocked transit.
  • Feedstock cost spike: Petrochemical feedstock costs up 15–25% globally; downstream impacts cascade through plastics, fertilizers, and pharmaceuticals.[44]
  • China exposure: Over 80% of China's petrochemical capacity is configured for Iranian crude grades. Iran's continued Kharg + Jask exports partially mitigate this, but any escalation halting Iranian exports triggers immediate cracker shutdowns across China's industrial northeast.[44]
  • HSFO crack spread: High-sulphur fuel oil (180 cst) widened +$20/bbl as bunker demand surges from longer rerouting distances. Cape route adds 10–14 days of fuel burn per voyage.[44]
  • European jet crack: Jet fuel premium to ICE gasoil hit all-time record of $518.5/t — first time ever above $500/t.[43]
  • US LPG: US produces 2.3 mb/d LPG with ~450K b/d spare export capacity. However, 5–6 week sailing time to Asia limits near-term relief — cargoes contracted today don't arrive until late April.[44]

Flow Rate Constraints: Why “Paper” Relief Lags Physical Reality

SPR releases and pipeline activations are announced in volume terms. The binding constraint is deployment rate — how fast the barrels can physically move. Headline numbers consistently overstate near-term relief.[6]

Demand Destruction Dynamics

  • Threshold price: Bernstein estimates $155/bbl Brent as the level triggering structural demand destruction — ~50% above current levels. Below this, oil demand is largely inelastic in the short run due to lack of substitutes for transportation and industrial use.
  • Price elasticity: Short-run oil demand elasticity: −0.2 to −0.3. At $103 Brent, implied demand reduction: ~2-3% globally (~2-3 mb/d). Meaningful, but far from closing the -12 mb/d structural gap.
  • IEA demand revisions: IEA raised its 2026 demand destruction estimate from 210K b/d to 640K b/d — reflecting higher-than-expected price pass-through to consumers and faster behavioral response in Asia.
  • Asian emergency measures: India mandating 10% ethanol blending expansion; Japan activating emergency conservation orders (factories, commercial buildings); South Korea issuing "reduce by 20%" public guidance; China restricting highway trucking to odd/even license plates in 7 provinces.
  • Stalemate scenario (15-20% demand drop): If closure extends 3+ months with no resolution, IEA models 15-20% demand contraction in Asia-Pacific — triggering a global recession that itself destroys demand. This is the self-correcting mechanism, but the adjustment is severe and non-linear.
  • Supply-demand clearing: Futures curve implies market expects clearing at ~$85 Brent by year-end (steep contango). Structural analysts (Pozsar, Trafigura) argue this assumes a brief disruption and is badly mispriced if closure extends past June.
