Iran's Selective Blockade Strategy

Iran is not enforcing a total closure. It is operating a selective blockade — blocking US/Western-allied shipping while allowing passage for strategically useful traffic. This divides the Western coalition (India and China have less incentive to act), maintains Iran's revenue, and turns Hormuz into a diplomatic lever.[6]

Repair & Restart Timelines

Physical Damage

  • Ras Tanura: 2-4 weeks (fire damage, equipment inspection)[5]
  • Ras Laffan LNG: 2 weeks restart + 2 weeks to full capacity; North Field expansion delayed to 2027[22]
  • Fujairah: Hit 3 times; loading impaired but ADCOP pipeline flowing at 1.8 mb/d
  • Others: Weeks to assess; fuel storage tank replacement 4-8 weeks minimum

Shut-in Wells

Water Coning Risk — Mar 28 Threshold Wells shut >4 weeks face permanent damage. Bottom water rushes into mature reservoirs during extended shut-ins, trapping oil in pore spaces — potentially irreversible formation damage. This is the under-discussed mechanism behind the 4-week threshold.[53] Wells shut since Mar 3 hit this window around Mar 28.
  • First 2 weeks: Restart straightforward (days to a week per well)
  • 2-4 weeks idle: Corrosion begins; inspection and equipment replacement needed
  • Beyond 4 weeks: Risk of permanent well damage — oil becomes trapped, potentially unrecoverable
  • IEA: "Upstream production will take weeks and, in some cases, months, to return to pre-crisis levels"[1]

Strait Reopening Scenarios

  • ING base case: 4 weeks total (2 full closure + 2 at 50%)
  • Bull case: Selective passage already occurring; US Navy estimates escort readiness by end of March (needs 8-10 destroyers per convoy); prerequisite per Bessent: "complete control of the skies and Iran's missile rebuilding capabilities completely degraded"[40]
  • Bear case: Khamenei's first statement confirmed Hormuz as leverage; Iran FM "we never asked for ceasefire"; could extend months

Mitigating Factors

1. Saudi East-West Pipeline (Petroline) STRUCTURAL

  • Pipe capacity: 7 mb/d (after NGL line conversion)
  • Yanbu throughput: ~2.5 mb/d (Sparta Commodities, Mar 16) vs 750K pre-crisis[10]
  • Net new export: +1.75 mb/d
  • Limitation: Only moves crude — no refined products, LPG, or LNG

2. UAE ADCOP Pipeline STRUCTURAL

  • Pipe capacity: 1.5-1.8 mb/d (now at full capacity)[10]
  • Spare activated: +0.7 mb/d
  • Risk: Fujairah hit 3 times but still operational

3. IEA Strategic Petroleum Reserve POLICY

  • Total committed: 400M bbl (largest in IEA's 50-year history)[11]
  • Structure: First tranche is 86M bbl exchange (loan; must return with interest)
  • Rate: ~2.0 mb/d (US: 1.43 mb/d over 120 days; others: 0.57 mb/d)
  • US pre-release level: 415.4M bbl[23]
  • Refill plan: Energy Sec. Wright: 200M bbl repurchased within one year
  • Market reaction: Flat. Brent held $100+ on announcement day.
  • Reality check: Actual delivered barrels not yet confirmed — physical pipeline lag from Gulf Coast caverns means first barrels only now reaching markets

4. GL 134: Russian Oil Waiver (One-Time) POLICY

Cliff Risk: Apr 11 GL 134 is a STOCK, not a FLOW. 186M bbl on ~238 tankers depletes and vanishes after expiration. No extension signals as of Mar 18 — legal analyses (Baker McKenzie, Thompson Hine) treat this as a one-time, cargo-specific reprieve with no automatic renewal.[45] This is the single most predictable near-term catalyst for price spikes.
  • OFAC GL 134: Authorizes delivery of Russian crude already loaded as of Mar 12[3]
  • Effective flow: ~2.0 mb/d tapering; 60-70M bbl near India/China for prompt delivery
  • Expires: April 11, 2026 (30 days)
  • Politics: Congressional Democrats call it "inexplicable"; Russia selling Urals at premium for first time since Ukraine invasion[48]

5. Other Mitigations

  • Russian production: +0.1 mb/d (minimal; Feb production was falling -56K bpd m/m; may cut 300K bpd by April per Rystad)
  • Iranian own exports: +1.5 mb/d via Kharg + Jask, almost entirely China-bound[24]
