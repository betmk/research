Mitigating Factors & Flow Rate Constraints

Announced relief ≠ delivered barrels. Physical deployment rates are constrained by pipeline bottlenecks, terminal capacity, and logistics. The paper balance sheet and physical market diverge significantly.

1. Saudi East-West Pipeline (Petroline) STRUCTURAL

  • Pipe capacity: 7 mb/d (after NGL line conversion). Yanbu throughput: ~2.5 mb/d (Sparta, Mar 16) vs 750K pre-crisis.
  • Net new export: +1.75 mb/d. Duration: indefinite. Limitation: crude only (no products, LPG, or LNG).[10]

2. UAE ADCOP Pipeline STRUCTURAL

  • Pipeline at full capacity (~1.8 mb/d, up from ~1.1 pre-crisis). Spare activated: +0.7 mb/d. Fujairah loading impaired but flowing.[8]

3. IEA SPR Release POLICY

  • 400M bbl total (largest in IEA’s 50-year history). Max rate: ~2.0 mb/d. First tranche: 86M bbl exchange (loan).[1]
  • US: 172M bbl at 1.43 mb/d (~120 days, exhausted ~Jul 10). Japan: 80M bbl. Germany: 19.7M bbl. UK: 13.5M bbl.[1]
  • Constraint: US cavern withdrawal maxes at 4.4 mb/d mechanically, but pipeline/terminal bottlenecks limit effective delivery to 1.43 mb/d committed rate.

4. GL 134: Russian Oil Waiver (One-Time) POLICY

  • ~186M bbl on ~238 laden tankers. 60–70M bbl near India/China deliverable. Effective flow: ~2.0 mb/d tapering to zero.[3]
  • Expires April 11 (3 days). No extension signals. Baker McKenzie: one-time, cargo-specific, no automatic renewal.
  • This is a STOCK, not a FLOW — it depletes and vanishes. The −2 mb/d cliff is the single most predictable near-term catalyst.

5. Other Supply Mitigations

  • Iranian own exports: +1.5 mb/d continuing via Kharg + Jask to China.[10]
  • Russian production: +0.1 mb/d (minimal; Feb output was falling −56K bpd m/m).
  • OECD commercial drawdown: ~1.0 mb/d effective draw rate from ~2.7B bbl stocks. Slows as stocks approach minimum operating levels (~60 days).
  • Demand destruction: IEA revised to 640K b/d; peaks ~0.8 mb/d in May then eases as prices fall (see Demand Destruction section).

Ceasefire Status: “Ceasefire” in Name Only (Apr 7–8)

The ceasefire is a label, not a reality. Both sides claim adherence while pursuing fundamentally irreconcilable objectives. Fighting has not stopped — it has shifted form. Violations were reported within hours. The market sold off 15% on the headline, but the underlying supply disruption is unchanged: Hormuz is still closed, mines are still in the water, 800+ ships are still stranded, and Iran retains vetting control of all transit.

Ongoing Hostilities Despite “Ceasefire”

  • Ceasefire violations within hours: UAE and Kuwait reported continued hostile activity on Apr 7.[117]
  • Iran’s position: Passage only “in coordination with Iranian Armed Forces” — Tehran treats the ceasefire as a pause in US bombing, not as a concession on Hormuz. The strait remains under Iranian operational control.[115]
  • Israel not bound: The ceasefire covers US bombing only. Israel has conducted independent strikes throughout the crisis (South Pars, Tehran, Nowruz strikes) and has not agreed to any pause.
  • Mines remain deployed: Iran has not announced any mine removal. The ~12 confirmed mines (Maham 3/7) make commercial transit physically unsafe regardless of political agreements.
  • Russia/China blocking: Vetoed Apr 7 UNSC resolution on commercial shipping protection — denying any multilateral enforcement mechanism for the ceasefire.[121]

Why the Market Sold Off Anyway (−15%)

Brent dropped from ~$112 to ~$95 on the ceasefire headline despite all of the above. This reflects headline-driven algorithmic selling, not a fundamental reassessment. The market is pricing the possibility of a deal at Islamabad, not the reality on the ground. If talks collapse, the snapback to $110+ is likely violent. BWET’s +7.5% move on ceasefire day — freight rising on a ceasefire — shows the physical market disagrees with the crude headline trade.[125]

What to Watch

Immediate Catalysts (next 10 days)

2. Ceasefire durability: 2-week window expires ~Apr 21. If no permanent deal, US resumes bombing and Brent reprices sharply. Watch for Iran’s 10-point demands vs US red lines.
3. Mojtaba Khamenei status: Injury/death rumors (Apr 1) unconfirmed. Resolution either way changes Iran’s negotiating posture.[112]
4. Mine clearance timeline: ~12 mines (Maham 3/7) confirmed. Even with a deal, physical clearing adds 7–14 days before commercial traffic resumes.

Structural Risks (weeks to months)

5. Well damage recovery: 41+ days idle. Every additional week adds recovery lag. Production recovery extends through July minimum. Futures curve (Dec 2026 ~$82) may be mispricing this.
6. SPR exhaustion: US portion (172M bbl) gone by mid-July at 1.43 mb/d. Post-SPR cliff if crisis extends past summer.
7. Ras Laffan LNG: 12.8M mt/yr offline for 3–5 years. Structural repricing of LNG futures beyond the crisis window.
8. Forward curve repricing: Back-end Brent at $79–85 assumes resolution by June. If Islamabad fails, Dec 2026 contracts reprice $10–15 higher.
9. Iraq fiscal crisis: Highest breakeven ($92), largest production cut (2.9 mb/d), minimal SWF. At sustained $85 post-crisis, Iraq runs deficit — political instability risk.

Background

10. Iran’s Hormuz protocol: Araghchi’s push for permanent IRGC-controlled transit. If institutionalized, structural reordering of energy transit rights.[19]
11. China’s reserve drawdown: Whether China is drawing or hoarding its ~1,400M bbl strategic stockpile is the most important unknown variable for crisis duration.
